Graduate Courses

  • (B+) PHYS701: Quantum Mechanics. Taught by Crystal Senko. Heisenberg and interaction pictures of quantum mechanics. Addition of angular momentum. Spherical tensor operators and the Wigner-Eckhart Theorem. Time dependent perturbation theory, the variatonal method, and the WKB approximation. The Aharanov-Bohm effect and geometric phase. Systems of identical particles with spin. Quantization of the electromagnetic field and first quantized quantum electrodynamics.
  • (A+) PHYS785: Quantum Field Theory for Cosmology. Taught by Achim Kempf. Quantization of Klein-Gordon field. Schrodinger and Fock representations of quantum field theory. Bogolubov transformations. Driving currents. Light-matter interaction. Unruh effect and detectors. Functional differentiation. Riemannian and Lorentzian structures. Einstein action and quantization. Darboux theorem. Free QFT on hyperbolic spacetime. Conservation and covariance of CCRs. Stone-Von Neumann theorem. Klein Gordon field in FRW spacetimes. Adiabatic vacuum. De Sitter horizon and inflation. Field fluctuation spectrum in expanding spacetime. Mukhanov variable and tensor polarizations. Schwarzchild spacetime. Boulware and Kruskal vacua. Hawking radiation. Path integral approach. Feynman diagrams.
  • (A+) PMATH965: Topics in Geometry and Topology - Gauge Theory. Taught by Ruxandra Moraru. Vector bundles and principal bundles (definitions and basic constructions); connections, curvature, and gauge groups. Ehresmann connections. Covariant derivatives and holonomy. Sheaves and Cech cohomology. Characteristic classes and Chern Weil Theory. Flat connections. Metric connections on vector bundles. Some important equations of gauge theory: Yang-Mills, anti-self-dual, Hermitian-Einstein.
  • *PMATH945: Topics in Algebra - Clifford Algebras, Spinors, and Calibrations. Taught by Spiro Karigiannis. Tensor products and exterior algebra; inner products, symplectic structures, and complex structures; normed real division algebras; cross products and calibrations; Clifford algebras and the related Spin groups; Jordan algebras. There are two main themes running through the second half of the course: Quaternions and octonions can be used to understand some specific structures in a concrete way, such as specific Spin groups, orthogonal groups, and Jordan algebras, in particular low dimensions. There is a way to generalize the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality from inner product spaces to spaces with calibrations using normed division algebras.

Physics

  • (A) PHYS234: Quantum Physics I. Taught by Raffi Budakian. Background of quantum physics. Introduction to formalism of quantum physics. Introduction to operators. Quantization, waves and particles. The uncertainty principle. The Schroedinger equation for one-dimensional problems: bound states in square wells. Harmonic oscillator; transmission through barriers.
  • (A) PHYS334: Quantum Physics II. Taught by Crystal Senko. Formalism of quantum mechanics. Operator approach to the harmonic oscillator. Quantum mechanics in three dimensions: Hydrogen atom, angular momentum and spin. Time-independent perturbation theory. Fine structure of hydrogen. Zeeman effect.
  • (A+) PHYS275: Planets. Taught by Avery Broderick. Orbital dynamics. Terrestrial and gas giant planets in the Solar System, asteroids and comets. Atmospheric physics. Extrasolar planets and astrobiology. Dynamics of star and planet formation.
  • (A) PHYS375: Stars. Taught by Avery Broderick. Stellar distances, masses, ages. Stellar interiors and atmospheres, the Saha Equation. Stellar equations of structure. Star formation and evolution. Cepheid Variables. Supernovae, white dwarfs, neutron stars, black holes.
  • (A+) PHYS358: Thermal Physics. Taught by Karen Cummings. Temperature and thermodynamic equilibrium. Work, internal energy and heat; first law, with examples. Kinetic theory of gases. Basic probability theory. Microscopic states and entropy. Absolute temperature, reversibility and the second law. Thermodynamic Functions and Maxwell's relations. Chemical potentials and free energy. Phase transitions.
  • (A) PHYS359: Statistical Mechanics. Taught by Jeff Chen. Phase space of quantum systems. Microcanonical ensemble. Paramagnets and ideal gases. Canonical ensemble. Free energy. Partition function. Classical and quantum ideal gases. Quantum paramagnetic systems. The Gibbs Paradox and multiparticle quantum physics. Grand Canonical ensemble and chemical potentials. Semiclassical correction to ideal gas. Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac distributions. Ideal electron gases and Fermi energy. Black body radiation. Bose-Einstein condensates. Virial coefficients.
  • (A) PHYS263: Classical Mechanics and Special Relativity. Taught by James Martin Newtonian dynamics of particles and systems of particles. Oscillations. Gravity and the central force problem. Lorentz transformations and relativistic dynamics.
  • (A+) PHYS363: Intermediate Classical Mechanics. Taught by Chris O'Donovan. Coupled oscillations and normal modes. Rigid Body Dynamics. Non-inertial frames of reference. Calculus of variations and d'Alambert's principle. Lagrangian mechanics (including rigid body mechanics and tops). Hamiltonian dynamics, the Poisson bracket, canonical transformations. Symplectomorphisms. Relativistic Lagrangian dynamics.
  • (B+) PHYS242: Electricity and Magnetism I. Taught by Adrian Lupascu. Electrostatics in vacuum, electric potential, conductors and currents, polarization of materials, electric fields in materials, LRC circuits.
  • (A) PHYS342: Electricity and Magnetism II. Taught by Matteo Mariantoni. Magnetic fields, Ampere's law, induced electromotive forces, magnetic devices, magnetic properties of materials, inductance, Electromagnetic waves and Poynting's theorem.
  • *PHYS442: Electricity and Magnetism III. Taught by Chris O'Donovan. Review of the origin of Maxwell's equations, conservation laws, potential formulation of Maxwell's equations and gauge choices, vacuum solutions of Maxwell's equations (free space, waveguides, dispersion), solution to Maxwell's equations for arbitrary sources (static and time-dependent problems), relativistic formulation of electrodynamics, macroscopic Maxwell's equations and plane waves in macroscopic media.

Pure Mathematics

  • (B) PMATH347: Groups and Rings. Taught by Matt Satriano. Groups, subgroups, homomorphisms and quotient groups, isomorphism theorems, group actions, Cayley and Lagrange theorems, permutation groups and the fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups. Elementary properties of rings, subrings, ideals, homomorphisms and quotients, isomorphism theorems, polynomial rings, and unique factorization domains. Noetherian rings.
  • (B+) PMATH365: Differential Geometry. Taught by Spiro Karigiannis. Exterior algebra of a vector space. Submanifolds of Euclidean n-space. Vector fields and differential forms. Integration on submanifolds and Stokes's Theorem. Hodge Star operator and vector calculus. De Rham Cohomology.
  • (A+) PMATH465: Smooth Manifolds. Taught by Stephen New. Topology of manifolds. Abstract manifolds, submanifolds. Submersions, immersions, and embeddings. Vector fields and differential forms. Abstract vector bundles. Lie groups and Lie algebras. Tensors. Lie Derivatives and Flows. De Rham Cohomology.
  • (A) PMATH333: Introduction to Real Analysis. Taught by Stephen New. The purpose of the course is to present the familiar concepts of calculus at a rigorous level and to provide students who took the MATH 137/MATH 138/MATH 237 sequence with the background needed to be successful in PMATH 351 and PMATH 352. Axiomatic set theory. Completeness properties of the reals; the density of the rationals; the topology of real n-dimensional space: open and closed sets, connectedness, compactness (by open covers), the Heine-Borel theorem, completeness; sequences in real n-dimensional space: convergence, Cauchy sequences, subsequences, the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem; multivariable functions: limits, point-wise and uniform continuity, the extreme value theorem, uniform convergence of sequences of functions, Taylor's theorem, term-by-term differentiation of power series; integration in real n-dimensional space: Riemann integrability, Fubini's theorem for continuous functions on rectangles, term-by-term integration of power series.
  • *PMATH351: Real Analysis. Taught by Stephen New. Cardinal Arithmetic. Normed and metric spaces, open sets, continuous mappings, sequence and function spaces, completeness, contraction mappings, compactness of metric spaces, infinite-dimensional normed and metric spaces, Arzela-Ascoli theorem, existence of solutions of differential equations, Stone-Weierstrass theorem. Lebesgue Outer Measure. Baire Category Theorems.
  • (A) PMATH352: Complex Analysis. Taught by Spiro Karigiannis. Greens' Theorem and identities. Analytic functions, Cauchy-Riemann equations, Goursat's theorem, Cauchy's theorems, Morera's theorem, Liouville's theorem, maximum modulus principle. Harmonic functions, Schwarz's lemma, isolated singularities, Laurent series, residue theorem.

Applied Mathematics

  • (C) AMATH331: Applied Real Analysis. Taught by Blake Madill. Topology of Euclidean spaces, continuity, norms, completeness. Contraction mapping principle. Banach spaces and infinite dimensional vector spaces. Fourier series. Various applications, for example, to ordinary differential equations, optimization and numerical approximation.
  • (B) AMATH351: Ordinary Differential Equations II. Taught by Xinzhi Liu. Second order linear differential equations with non-constant coefficients, Sturm comparison, oscillation and separation theorems, series solutions and special functions. Linear vector differential equations in Rn, an introduction to dynamical systems. Laplace transforms applied to linear vector differential equations, transfer functions, the convolution theorem. Green's functions. Sturm-Liouville Theory. Stability of inhomogenous and nonlinear systems.
  • (A+) AMATH353: Partial Differential Equations I. Taught by Francis Poulin. Second order linear partial differential equations and classification of second order partial differential equations. Methods of solution - separation of variables and eigenfunction expansions, the Fourier transform. Henkel transforms and Laplace transforms. Emphasis on Sturm-Liouville theory and differential operators. Physical interpretation of solutions in terms of diffusion, waves and steady states. First order non-linear partial differential equations and the method of characteristics. Quasilinear partial differential equations and shock formation. Uniqueness theorems for Hyperbolic boundary value problems.
  • *AMATH463: Fluid Mechanics. Taught by Marek Stastna. Incompressible, irrotational flow. Incompressible viscous flow. Introduction to wave motion and geophysical fluid mechanics. Elements of compressible flow.
  • *AMATH473: Quantum Theory II. Taught by Achim Kempf. The Hilbert space of states, observables, and time evolution. Feynman path integral and Greens functions. Approximation methods. Co-ordinate transformations, angular momentum, and spin. The relation between symmetries and conservation laws. Density matrix, Ehrenfest theorem, and decoherence. Multiparticle quantum mechanics. Bell inequality and basics of quantum computing.
  • (B) STAT230: Probability. Taught by S. Banerjee. This course provides an introduction to probability models including sample spaces, mutually exclusive and independent events, conditional probability and Bayes' Theorem. The named distributions (Discrete Uniform, Hypergeometric, Binomial, Negative Binomial, Geometric, Poisson, Continuous Uniform, Exponential, Normal (Gaussian), and Multinomial) are used to model real phenomena. Discrete and continuous univariate random variables and their distributions are discussed. Joint probability functions, marginal probability functions, and conditional probability functions of two or more discrete random variables and functions of random variables are also discussed. Students learn how to calculate and interpret means, variances and covariances particularly for the named distributions. The Central Limit Theorem is used to approximate probabilities.
  • *STAT231: Statistics. Taught by S. Banerjee. This course provides a systematic approach to empirical problem solving which will enable students to critically assess the sampling protocol and conclusions of an empirical study including the possible sources of error in the study and whether evidence of a causal relationship can be reasonably concluded. The connection between the attributes of a population and the parameters in the named distributions covered in STAT 230 will be emphasized. Numerical and graphical techniques for summarizing data and checking the fit of a statistical model will be discussed. The method of maximum likelihood will be used to obtain point and interval estimates for the parameters of interest as well as testing hypotheses. The interpretation of confidence intervals and p-values will be emphasized. The Chi-squared and t distributions will be introduced and used to construct confidence intervals and tests of hypotheses including likelihood ratio tests. Contingency tables and Gaussian response models including the two sample Gaussian and simple linear regression will be used as examples.
